### Custodial Account Rules (Virginia)

- **UTMA/UGMA**: Can be opened at birth. No earned income required. Parent is custodian until age 21 (Virginia UTMA). Contributions are irrevocable gifts.
- **Custodial Roth IRA**: Requires earned income (W-2 or legitimate self-employment). Practically available age 14–16+. Contribution limit = lesser of $7,000 or total earned income.
- **Purpose**: The custodial account is a *teaching tool*, not just a savings vehicle. A $100 account the child actively watches and discusses is worth more educationally than a $50,000 529 they never think about.

**Discipline** — Can they:
- Delay gratification?
- Save money?
- Finish hard things?

### Character Case Studies: Lives as Text

> This is not propaganda or value installation. It is raw analysis of oneself using others as mirrors.

Study controversial and contemporary figures — not to idolize or condemn, but to examine how their actions align with or violate the sacramental ladder at each level (individual, family, community, nation). Every figure contains both — actions that serve and actions that damage.

**The method:**
1. What era did they live in? What pressures shaped them?
2. What did they sacrifice? What did they destroy?
3. Where did their actions align with the sacramental ladder?
4. Where did they violate it?
5. What can be learned without adopting everything?
6. What in myself mirrors their strengths? Their failures?

**Seed figures (expand with age):**

| Figure | Why Study Them |
|--------|---------------|
| Martin Luther King Jr. | Nonviolent resistance, prophetic speech, personal moral failures, cost of conviction |
| Malcolm X | Transformation, self-education, initial rage vs. later reconciliation, intellectual honesty |
| Daryl Davis | Engaging the enemy with presence rather than argument, courage without violence |
| David Goggins | Suffering as forge, discipline as identity, risk of self-destruction disguised as strength |
| Sebastian Junger | Tribe, belonging, what men need, the cost of modern isolation |

**Age-appropriate progression:**
- **Age 11–13:** Introduce one figure per year. Focus on what they faced and what they chose.
- **Age 14–16:** Compare two figures on the same issue (e.g., MLK vs. Malcolm X on responding to injustice). Neither is "right" — analyze both.
- **Age 16–18:** Full sacramental ladder analysis. Where did this person build up the ladder? Where did they tear it down? Apply to self: "What in me is like this?"
- **Age 18–25:** Encounter more complex figures. Apply the framework independently. The goal: the child can analyze *any* public figure's life without needing to be told what to think.

**The guardrail:** This is discernment, not hero worship. Every figure studied must be examined for *both* their contributions and their damage. If you only see the good, you're doing propaganda. If you only see the bad, you're doing cynicism. The sacramental ladder provides the framework for honest analysis.

> See **Document 07 — Spiritual Warfare & Discernment** for the analytical framework (projection, transference, conviction vs. accusation) applied to these case studies.

## The Core Metric

The most important metric on the entire chart is not grades, income, or career prestige.

It is:

> **"Is this child becoming a wise, capable, kind, and independent person?"**

Everything else — college, trade school, military service, entrepreneurship, software engineering, medicine, teaching — is downstream of that.
