## Stage 0: Infancy to Early Toddler Years (0–3 Years)

**Objective:** Lay a financial foundation to support future education and introduce basic concepts of financial planning to contributing family members.

### Activities

#### 529 Plan Establishment

- Open a 529 college savings plan to begin investing in the child's future education. This tax-advantaged savings plan allows for tax-free growth of investments if used for eligible education expenses, including college, trade schools, and even K-12 tuition in some cases.
- Parents or guardians should familiarize themselves with the rules regarding contributions, growth, and withdrawals to ensure the plan's benefits are fully realized. This includes understanding annual contribution limits, gift tax rules, and eligible expenses.
- Explore state-specific benefits, such as tax deductions or credits for contributions, to maximize financial advantages.

#### Family Financial Briefing

Prepare a briefing document to share with relatives and potential contributors (e.g., grandparents, aunts, uncles). This document should outline:

- The purpose of the plan and how it supports the child's future education.
- Contribution options and how family members can make deposits directly into the account.
- The importance of adhering to contribution limits to avoid tax penalties.
- Information on potential state tax deductions for contributions.

Set up a meeting or virtual discussion to present the briefing, ensuring that all contributing relatives are aware of how the plan works and how they can be part of the child's financial support network.

#### Tracking and Accountability

- Regularly monitor the progress of the 529 plan, tracking contributions, investment growth, and market performance. Ensure that the plan remains aligned with long-term educational goals.
- Hold periodic check-ins with contributing family members to provide updates on the plan's progress, discuss any changes in contribution strategies, and answer questions.
- Encourage relatives to communicate any changes in their financial ability to contribute so that adjustments can be made without strain or miscommunication.

> By establishing a financial foundation early on, this stage ensures that the future educational needs of the child are supported, while also fostering a culture of financial awareness and planning among the family.

## Stage 1: Early Conversational Age (3–6 Years)

**Objective:** Introduce the concept of varied activities and professions through play and storytelling.

### Activities

- **Storytime:** Use children's books featuring characters with different jobs to expose the child to a variety of professions in a fun and engaging way.
- **Play:** Encourage play that involves various roles (doctor, teacher, artist) to stimulate interest in different professions.
- **Observation:** Discuss the jobs of family members, friends, and characters on TV shows or in books, helping the child connect everyday life to different careers.

---

## Stage 2: Elementary Years (7–10 Years)

**Objective:** Foster curiosity and basic understanding of personal interests and skills.

### Activities

- **Exploration Clubs:** Enroll in after-school clubs or activities (science, art, sports) to explore a range of interests and talents.
- **Career Days:** Participate in or organize events with guest speakers from different professions to broaden the child's awareness of potential career paths.
- **Skills Assessment:** Use simple assessments or conversations to identify strengths and preferences, encouraging early self-awareness.

---

## Stage 3: Pre-Adolescence (11–13 Years)

**Objective:** Deepen understanding of interests and introduce the concept of career paths.

### Activities

- **Research Projects:** Assign small projects to research different professions, encouraging the child to explore careers that align with their developing interests.
- **Mentorship:** Seek opportunities for short-term mentorship or shadowing experiences in areas of interest to provide real-world exposure.
- **Self-Reflection:** Encourage journaling or discussions about personal goals, interests, and potential career aspirations.

---

## Stage 4: Early Adolescence (14–16 Years)

**Objective:** Begin strategic planning for future education and career paths.

### Activities

- **Career Counseling:** Access school or community career counseling services to start aligning educational paths with career goals.
- **Academic Planning:** Choose high school courses with future goals in mind, ensuring that they support the child's career aspirations.
- **Work Experience:** Look for part-time jobs, internships, or volunteer opportunities related to areas of interest, helping the adolescent gain practical experience.

---

## Stage 5: Late Adolescence (17–18 Years)

**Objective:** Finalize decisions regarding post-secondary education or training and career paths.

### Activities

- **College and Career Fairs:** Attend to gather information and network with professionals, aiding in making informed decisions about future education or career choices.
- **Application Assistance:** Seek help with college, scholarship, or job applications to ensure that the process is as smooth as possible.
- **Decision Making:** Support in evaluating options and making informed decisions that align with both personal goals and career aspirations.

---

## Guiding Principles Throughout All Stages

- **Informational Awareness:** Constantly gather and discuss information about different careers, focusing on changes in the job market and emerging professions.
- **Self-Awareness and Flexibility:** Emphasize the importance of understanding oneself, including interests, strengths, and values, while remaining flexible to change.
- **Decision-Making Support:** Provide support in decision-making processes without dictating choices, ensuring that the individual feels in control of their future.
- **Environmental Resilience:** At every stage, pair career exploration with awareness of that field's pressures, failure modes, and substance risks. See **Document 06 — Environmental Awareness & Resilience** for the full stage-by-stage resilience mapping.
