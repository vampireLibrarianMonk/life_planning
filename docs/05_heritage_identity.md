### Language Comprehension Pathway

Language acquisition is not a single skill. It is a layered system. Each layer builds on the one below. Gaps at lower layers undermine everything above.

#### Layer 1: Phonological Awareness (Sound System)

The ability to hear, distinguish, and produce the sounds of the language.

| Stage | Indicator | Age Window |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **Sound exposure** | Hears the language regularly in natural speech | 0–6 months |
| **Sound discrimination** | Distinguishes target language phonemes from dominant language | 6–12 months |
| **Babbling in target** | Produces sounds specific to target language (e.g., rolled R, ñ) | 8–14 months |
| **Phonemic awareness** | Can identify beginning/ending sounds of words | 3–5 years |
| **Accent integration** | Produces sounds without dominant-language accent | Before age 7 (critical period) |

**Critical note:** After approximately age 7, native-like phonology becomes significantly harder to acquire. Exposure before this window is not optional for native pronunciation.

#### Layer 2: Vocabulary (Lexical Development)

Raw word knowledge — both receptive (understands) and productive (uses).

| Stage | Receptive (Understands) | Productive (Uses) | Approximate Age |
|-------|------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Core nouns** | Body, food, family, animals, home | ~50 words | 12–24 months |
| **Verbs & actions** | Understands commands and narration | 2-word combinations | 18–30 months |
| **Descriptors** | Adjectives, adverbs, prepositions | 3–4 word sentences | 2–3 years |
| **Concrete vocabulary** | 2,000+ words across daily domains | Tells simple stories | 3–5 years |
| **Academic vocabulary** | Content words for school subjects | Explains concepts | 6–10 years |
| **Abstract vocabulary** | Emotions, philosophy, speculation | Argues and persuades | 10–14 years |
| **Technical/professional** | Specialized domain language | Functions professionally | 14–18+ years |
| **Nuanced/literary** | Connotation, register, poetry, double meaning | Writes with sophistication | 16+ years |

**Vocabulary domains to track:**

| Domain | Examples |
|--------|----------|
| Home/daily life | Kitchen, bathroom, chores, routines |
| Family/relationships | Kinship terms, emotions, conflict resolution |
| Nature/environment | Weather, animals, geography, seasons |
| School/academics | Math terms, science terms, instructions |
| Body/health | Body parts, feelings, sickness, doctor visits |
| Commerce/money | Buying, selling, numbers, transactions |
| Time/sequence | Days, months, before/after, cause/effect |
| Abstract/moral | Right/wrong, fairness, duty, faith |
| Social/cultural | Greetings, formality, humor, slang |
| Professional | Career-specific terminology |

#### Layer 3: Grammar (Morphosyntax)

Understanding and producing the structural rules of the language.

| Stage | What It Looks Like | Approximate Age |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Word order** | Correct subject-verb-object (or target language order) | 2–3 years |
| **Basic conjugation** | Present tense verbs with correct endings | 2.5–4 years |
| **Plurals & gender** | Correct noun agreement (el/la, -os/-as) | 3–5 years |
| **Past tense** | Preterite used correctly in narration | 4–6 years |
| **Subjunctive/conditional** | Complex mood usage (quiero que vayas) | 6–10 years |
| **Relative clauses** | Embedded sentences (el libro que leí) | 7–11 years |
| **Formal register** | Tú vs. usted; appropriate verb forms for context | 8–12 years |
| **Academic grammar** | Complex sentence structures, passive voice, nominalizations | 12–16 years |
| **Literary grammar** | Stylistic variation, historical forms, rhetorical structures | 16+ years |

**Common grammar gaps in heritage speakers:**

These are predictable and not signs of failure — they're signs of limited formal input:
- Gender agreement errors (common even to age 10–12 in bilinguals)
- Simplified verb tenses (using present for everything)
- Code-switching at grammar boundaries (inserting English structure into Spanish words)
- Reduced subjunctive use (replaced by indicative)

Each gap identifies where more input — not correction — is needed.

#### Layer 4: Discourse (Connected Language)

The ability to produce and understand language beyond individual sentences.

| Stage | Indicator | Approximate Age |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Turn-taking** | Responds appropriately in conversation | 2–3 years |
| **Simple narration** | Tells what happened (beginning → end) | 3–5 years |
| **Sequenced narration** | Tells stories with temporal order and cause/effect | 5–7 years |
| **Descriptive discourse** | Describes scenes, people, processes in detail | 7–9 years |
| **Argumentative discourse** | States opinion, gives reasons, anticipates objections | 9–12 years |
| **Expository discourse** | Explains concepts, teaches others, summarizes | 12–15 years |
| **Persuasive discourse** | Convinces, negotiates, advocates in target language | 14–18 years |
| **Academic/professional discourse** | Presents, writes papers, conducts meetings | 18+ years |

#### Layer 5: Pragmatics (Social Use)

Knowing what to say, to whom, when, and how.

| Stage | Indicator | Approximate Age |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Basic politeness** | Please, thank you, greetings in target language | 2–4 years |
| **Context awareness** | Knows when to use target language vs. dominant | 4–6 years |
| **Register switching** | Speaks differently to grandparent vs. cousin | 6–9 years |
| **Humor** | Understands and produces jokes, wordplay, sarcasm | 8–12 years |
| **Indirect speech** | Understands hints, implied meaning, understatement | 10–14 years |
| **Cultural scripts** | Knows how to behave in cultural contexts (church, quinceañera, funeral) | 12–16 years |
| **Professional register** | Knows formal vs. informal in workplace/academic settings | 16+ years |
| **Full cultural fluency** | Navigates any social situation without marking as "foreign" | 18+ years |

#### Layer 6: Literacy (Reading & Writing)

Separate from oral language — requires explicit instruction.

| Stage | Reading | Writing | Approximate Age |
|-------|---------|---------|-----------------|
| **Pre-literacy** | Recognizes that text carries meaning; knows letters | Scribbles with intent; writes name | 3–5 years |
| **Decoding** | Sounds out words; reads simple sentences | Writes words phonetically | 5–7 years |
| **Fluency** | Reads paragraph-level text without stopping | Writes sentences with correct spelling | 7–9 years |
| **Comprehension** | Reads chapter books; infers meaning from context | Writes paragraphs with structure | 9–11 years |
| **Analysis** | Reads for subtext, bias, argument; identifies author's purpose | Writes essays with thesis and evidence | 11–14 years |
| **Synthesis** | Reads across sources; compares perspectives | Writes research, creative, or professional pieces | 14–17 years |
| **Mastery** | Reads literature, technical texts, historical documents fluently | Writes at professional/academic level | 17+ years |

**Literacy note:** Oral fluency does NOT guarantee literacy. A child who speaks Spanish fluently at home may still need explicit reading/writing instruction in Spanish. Do not assume transfer.

### Comprehension vs. Production Gap

In bilingual development, comprehension *always* leads production. A child who understands but won't speak is not failing — they are in the **silent period** or have a comprehension-production gap.

| Pattern | What It Means | Response |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Understands but responds in English | Normal; dominant language is easier to produce | Increase input; don't force output |
| Speaks in mixed code | Using both grammars creatively; sign of bilingual competence | Model full sentences; don't correct mixing |
| Refuses to speak target language | Social pressure, embarrassment, or insufficient peer models | Create low-pressure contexts; connect with same-language peers |
| Understands parents but not media/strangers | Limited to familiar voices and topics | Diversify input sources (audiobooks, shows, extended family calls) |
| Reads in English but not target language | Literacy wasn't explicitly taught in target language | Start literacy instruction — it won't transfer automatically |

---

### The Attrition Warning

Language attrition (loss) follows a predictable pattern:

```
Reduced exposure → Vocabulary shrinks first
  → Grammar simplifies (uses present for everything)
    → Comprehension narrows to familiar topics
      → Production stops (but comprehension may remain)
        → "I understand Grandma but can't talk back"
          → Heritage language becomes passive/receptive only
```

**Prevention:** Maintain minimum 20–30% exposure through adulthood. The language must remain *useful* — not just sentimental.

### Family Participation Roles

Language development cannot rest on one person. Assign roles:

| Role | Who | Responsibility |
|------|-----|---------------|
| **Primary speaker** | Parent/grandparent | Speaks target language consistently with child (not mixing) |
| **Reinforcer** | Other parent | Understands and supports even if not fluent; never discourages |
| **Immersion source** | Extended family, media, community | Provides natural context (trips, calls, books, shows, church) |
| **Literacy guide** | Tutor, parent, school | Teaches reading/writing in target language |
| **Culture carrier** | Grandparents, community | Connects language to stories, food, music, traditions |
| **Peer connector** | Parents/community | Provides same-age peers who speak the language |

**The OPOL Principle (One Parent One Language):**

If possible, each parent speaks a different language consistently. The child learns both as "natural" rather than one as "extra." If both parents share a primary language, assign contexts instead:
- Home = Spanish
- School/outside = English
- Or: Mornings = Spanish, school pickup onward = English

### Tracking

**Monthly assessment questions:**

1. How many hours/week of target language exposure this month?
2. Which vocabulary domains are strong? Which have gaps?
3. Is the child producing or only comprehending?
4. Is any family member slipping into dominant language?
5. What new grammar structures appeared (or should appear by now)?
6. Are there peer interactions in the target language?
7. Is literacy progressing or stalled?

**The Key Principle:**

> Language is not a subject to be studied. It is an environment to be lived in. The family creates the environment, or the language dies in one generation.

## The Final Answer

The answer isn't ultimately a DNA percentage or a family tree.

The final answer becomes:

> **"What forces, people, beliefs, sacrifices, languages, and stories made me who I am, and what part of that inheritance am I responsible for carrying forward?"**
