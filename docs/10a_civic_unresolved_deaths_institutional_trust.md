# Civic Extension Addendum: Unresolved Deaths, Official Narratives & Institutional Trust
## Repeatable Civic Bounty Track for the Life Planning App

This markdown is an addendum to the Civic pillar and the Jury Duty / Controversial Case Review bounty system.

It covers a different type of civic case: unresolved or disputed public deaths where there may be no clean jury verdict, no complete public record, sealed documents, conflicting official theories, or long-term public suspicion.

This is the kind of issue represented by cases such as *The Midnight Ride of Jonathan Luna*.

The purpose is not to teach the child to become conspiratorial.

The purpose is to teach disciplined uncertainty, institutional literacy, source evaluation, and civic seriousness when the official record is incomplete or disputed.

# 2. Why This Is Separate From a Jury Bounty

A normal jury bounty works best when there is:

- a trial
- charges
- a judge
- jury instructions
- admitted evidence
- a verdict
- a clear legal question

But some civic controversies do not have that structure.

Some cases involve:

- unresolved deaths
- disputed official narratives
- sealed records
- conflicting investigative theories
- disagreements between agencies
- family objections
- media investigations
- public suspicion
- incomplete forensic records
- classified or withheld documents
- no prosecution
- no trial
- no final public answer

Those cases require a different civic skill.

The child is not acting as a juror.

The child is acting as a disciplined citizen reviewing a public mystery, an institutional trust problem, or an unresolved official narrative.

---

# 3. Plain-English Purpose

This bounty teaches the child how to study a serious public case without pretending to know more than the evidence supports.

The child learns to ask:

```text
What is known?
What is alleged?
What is officially concluded?
What is disputed?
What is missing?
What is sealed?
What is speculative?
What cannot be responsibly claimed?
```

The goal is not to solve the case.

The goal is to understand the relationship between:

- evidence
- institutions
- public trust
- secrecy
- journalism
- law enforcement
- official narratives
- forensic findings
- civic responsibility

---

# 4. Core Principle

A citizen should not confuse mystery with proof.

A citizen should also not confuse official silence with certainty.

The child must learn to say:

```text
This is known.
This is alleged.
This is officially concluded.
This is disputed.
This is sealed or missing.
This is plausible but unproven.
This is speculation.
This is unknowable from the current record.
```

This is disciplined uncertainty.

# 6. Guardrails

## Do Not Teach Conspiracy Thinking

The child should not be rewarded for inventing a theory.

Suspicion is not proof.

A strange fact is not a conclusion.

A missing record is not automatic evidence of guilt.

## Do Not Teach Blind Institutional Trust

The child should not be told that official conclusions are always correct.

Institutions can be mistaken, self-protective, incompetent, corrupt, politically pressured, or incomplete.

## Do Not Teach Cynicism

The child should not assume every unexplained case proves a hidden plot.

Sometimes cases remain unresolved because of poor evidence, bad luck, human error, jurisdiction problems, missing witnesses, or ordinary investigative limits.

## Do Not Teach Character Assassination

The child should avoid accusing specific people of crimes without evidence.

The project should focus on records, institutions, timelines, and evidence quality.

## Do Not Teach Certainty Where There Is None

If the case remains unresolved, the child should say so clearly.

# 10. Source Discipline Categories

The child must separate information into clear categories.

## Confirmed Fact

A fact supported by strong records, official documents, forensic evidence, or multiple reliable sources.

## Official Finding

A conclusion made by a coroner, medical examiner, court, law enforcement agency, or other authority.

## Investigative Theory

A theory apparently favored by investigators, which may or may not match the official finding.

## Allegation

A claim made by a person, family member, journalist, official, witness, or commentator.

## Disputed Claim

A claim contradicted by other sources or challenged by evidence.

## Missing Record

A record that would matter but is not currently available.

## Sealed Record

A record known to exist but not publicly accessible.

## Speculation

A possible explanation not proven by the available record.

## Public Narrative

The way the case is commonly described by the public.

## Media Narrative

The way the case is framed by books, podcasts, documentaries, or reporting.

## Personal Reaction

The child’s own response, clearly labeled as personal reaction rather than proof.

# 11. Source Reliability Ranking

Use this ranking in the app.

## Level 1: Primary Official Record

Examples:

- autopsy report
- medical examiner report
- coroner report
- court filing
- official docket
- official investigative report
- hearing transcript
- official agency document
- official death certificate
- official press release

## Level 2: Primary Supporting Material

Examples:

- public records request release
- FOIA release
- official maps
- surveillance logs
- phone records
- police radio records
- verified forensic records
- verified photographs
- official correspondence

## Level 3: Serious Secondary Source

Examples:

- long-form investigative journalism
- book with citations
- documentary using records
- law review article
- university case study
- reputable news investigation

## Level 4: Interested-Party Source

Examples:

- family statement
- attorney statement
- agency spokesperson
- advocacy group
- political figure
- witness interview
- podcast interview

These may be useful, but interest and perspective must be noted.

## Level 5: Public Narrative / Commentary

Examples:

- social media posts
- viral clips
- unsourced claims
- message boards
- political commentary
- speculation threads
- anonymous claims

These should never be treated as the main record.

# 16. Comparison With Jury Review Bounty

| Feature | Jury Review Bounty | Unresolved Death / Institutional Trust Bounty |
|---|---|---|
| Main role | Juror | Disciplined citizen / civic investigator |
| Core question | Was the legal burden met? | What can responsibly be known? |
| Best source | Trial record | Official records, forensic reports, public records |
| Main danger | Mob judgment | Conspiracy thinking or blind trust |
| Output | Juror conclusion | Known / unknown / disputed map |
| Legal endpoint | Verdict or court ruling | Often no clear endpoint |
| Skill trained | Legal reasoning | Institutional trust and disciplined uncertainty |

# 23. Final Principle

Some civic cases do not teach the child how to decide guilt.

They teach the child how to live with incomplete knowledge.

A responsible citizen must be able to say:

```text
I do not know enough to solve this.

But I can identify what is known, what is disputed, what is missing, what institutions did, and what questions remain.
```

The goal is not paranoia.

The goal is not blind trust.

The goal is disciplined uncertainty, civic patience, and mature judgment.
