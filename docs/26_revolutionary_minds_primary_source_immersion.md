# Revolutionary Minds: Primary Source Immersion Program
## Getting Inside the Head of a Decision-Maker in Extremis

---

## Purpose

Most people learn about revolutionary figures through textbooks that already tell them what to think. The textbook says "Washington was a hero" or "Mao was a tyrant" — and the child absorbs a conclusion without ever encountering the actual mind that made the decisions.

This program inverts that. The child reads **primary sources** — the letters, speeches, manifestos, diaries, and published documents the person actually wrote — and must evaluate for themselves:

- What was the situation they faced?
- What options did they actually have?
- What did they choose and why?
- What did they expect to happen?
- What actually happened — immediately, in 10 years, in 50 years?
- Did the net outcome justify the cost?
- Who paid the cost? Who reaped the benefit?
- Would I have done the same thing in their position?

**This is not hero worship. This is not condemnation. This is evaluation.**

---

## Core Principle

**Get into their head. Then get back out and judge by the fruit.**

The child must do both:
1. **Empathy:** Understand the decision-maker's context, constraints, information, pressures, and reasoning as they experienced them — not as a modern observer with hindsight
2. **Evaluation:** Step back out and assess the actual outcomes with the Fruit Test — what did this person's decisions actually produce over time?

A child who can only do #1 becomes an apologist. A child who can only do #2 becomes a smug judge. Both are useless. The program demands both.

## Program Structure

### Phase 1: Orientation (Bronze, 13–18)

Before reading anyone's primary documents, the child learns the evaluation framework:

- What is a primary source vs. a secondary source vs. commentary?
- How to read a historical document in context (not with modern assumptions)
- The Fruit Test applied to historical figures: what did they produce?
- The empathy requirement: understand before judging
- The evaluation requirement: judge after understanding
- The danger of both hero worship and reflexive condemnation

**Bounty:** Framework Study — read about primary source methodology and produce a one-page guide for how you will evaluate the figures you study.

### Phase 1B: Wandering — Rapid Survey (Bronze, 13–18)

Before committing an entire summer to one figure, the child browses widely. This is a rapid-fire sampling phase where they taste 5–8 figures in short bursts and discover which mind actually pulls them in.

**Cadence:** 1–2 days per figure. Read ONE short primary document from each (a single letter, one speech, one chapter). Not enough to evaluate — enough to feel the voice.

**Purpose:**
- Discover which figures they're drawn to (and which bore or repel them)
- Build awareness of how many different kinds of revolutionary minds exist
- Prevent the parent from assigning a summer of reading the child doesn't care about
- Generate genuine curiosity before the commitment of immersion

**Suggested sampling texts (one per figure, short):**
- Washington: Farewell Address (20 min read)
- Bolívar: Letter from Jamaica, opening pages
- Paine: First 5 pages of *Common Sense*
- Gandhi: One chapter from *Experiments with Truth*
- Mandela: Rivonia Trial speech ("I am prepared to die")
- Frederick Douglass: Chapter 1 of the *Narrative*
- Mao: "Report on the Peasant Movement in Hunan" (opening)
- Havel: Opening of *The Power of the Powerless*
- Che: One diary entry from *Motorcycle Diaries*
- Churchill: "We shall fight on the beaches" speech
- MLK: *Letter from Birmingham Jail* (first 3 pages)
- Lenin: One chapter of *State and Revolution*
- Gene Sharp: First chapter of *From Dictatorship to Democracy*

**Deliverable:** A ranked list — "These 3 grabbed me, these bored me, this one made me angry, this one confused me." With one sentence explaining *why* for each.

**The rule:** The child chooses their summer immersion figure from this list. Not the parent. The parent has veto power for age-appropriateness, but the pull must come from the child. A summer of forced reading produces compliance, not formation.

**Bounty:** Wandering Survey — sample at least 6 figures, produce ranked list with reasoning, select summer figure.

### Phase 2: First Immersion — Summer Reading (Silver, 13–18)

The child selects one figure (parent-approved) and reads their primary documents daily for 4–8 weeks. Not a biography. Not a textbook. The person's own words.

**Daily cadence:** 30–60 minutes of reading. Weekly reflection journal entry (what surprised me, what I noticed about their reasoning, what I disagree with, what I respect).

**End-of-summer deliverable:** A fact sheet covering:
- Context: What was the situation? What were the constraints?
- Options: What choices did they actually have? (Not what WE think they should have done with hindsight)
- Decision: What did they choose and why (in their own words)?
- Immediate outcome: What happened in the first 1–5 years?
- Long-term outcome: What happened in 10–50 years?
- Net assessment: Did it work? For whom? At what cost?
- Fruit Test: What kind of society/movement/institution did their work produce?
- Personal reflection: What would I have done? What do I admire? What do I condemn?

**Bounty:** First Immersion Complete — summer reading + fact sheet + family presentation.

### Phase 3: Second Immersion — Contrasting Figure (Gold, 13–18)

The child reads a second figure who faced a similar situation but chose differently, or who operated in a contrasting context. The comparison reveals that different choices produce different fruit.

Examples of pairs:
- Washington (revolution → republic) vs. Bolívar (revolution → fragmentation)
- Gandhi (nonviolence → partition) vs. Mandela (imprisonment → reconciliation)
- Mao (revolution → catastrophic governance) vs. Deng Xiaoping (reform → economic miracle + continued authoritarianism)
- Frederick Douglass (rhetoric → abolition) vs. John Brown (violence → martyrdom)
- Ho Chi Minh (anti-colonial) vs. Aquino (people power)
- Churchill (wartime leadership) vs. de Gaulle (exile → return)
- Lenin (revolution → totalitarianism) vs. Havel (dissidence → democratic transition)

**Deliverable:** Comparative analysis — same framework, two figures, then a comparison section: what explains the different outcomes?

**Bounty:** Comparative Immersion — second summer reading + comparative fact sheet.

### Phase 4: Synthesis — Patterns Across Figures (Platinum, 13–18)

After studying 2+ figures, the child identifies patterns:

- What conditions produce successful revolutions vs. failed ones?
- What personal characteristics correlate with positive long-term outcomes?
- When does violence produce freedom? When does it produce worse tyranny?
- What role does the day-after-victory governance plan play?
- What separates a liberator from a future dictator?
- Is there a pattern for when revolutionaries become the thing they fought?

**Deliverable:** Pattern analysis essay + family discussion.

**Bounty:** Synthesis and Patterns — cross-figure analysis identifying structural lessons.

### Phase 5: Application — What Would You Do? (Diamond, 18–25)

The child is presented with a real historical scenario (or a carefully constructed composite) and must produce their own decision framework:

- Given these constraints, these resources, this opposition, this population — what would you do?
- What are the second and third order consequences of your plan?
- How do you prevent becoming what you're fighting?
- What does your governance plan look like for the day after victory?
- Apply the Fruit Test to your own hypothetical: what would your plan produce in 50 years?

**Deliverable:** Decision paper + family presentation.

**Bounty:** Application and Decision — produces a reasoned response to an impossible situation.

## Guardrails

### Do not romanticize violence
Some revolutions required violence. Some produced freedom through it. But the child must always ask: "At what cost? To whom? And what came after?" The fact that violence sometimes works does not make it automatically righteous.

### Do not condemn all revolution
Sometimes the existing order is worse than the disruption. The child must not be taught "obey always." They must be taught: "What are the conditions that make revolt legitimate? What evidence would I need? What alternatives have been exhausted?"

### Do not skip the aftermath
Anyone can tear down. The question is: what do you build? The child must evaluate not just the revolutionary moment but the 10–50 year aftermath. Mao's revolution "succeeded" — then produced the Great Famine and the Cultural Revolution. The American Revolution "succeeded" — then produced a republic that took another 89 years to end slavery. No revolution is evaluated by its opening act alone.

### Do not allow the child to become a radical in either direction
This program should produce a child who can think like a revolutionary (empathy) AND judge like a historian (evaluation). If it produces a child who wants to burn things down, it has failed. If it produces a child who believes all authority is legitimate and revolt is never justified, it has also failed.

### Parent selects figures and supervises reading
Not all figures are appropriate for all ages. Mein Kampf at 14 is too early. Che at 13 without context is dangerous. The parent curates the reading list and discusses weekly.

### Label source types clearly
- Primary source (the person's own words)
- Memoir (retrospective — they're telling their story after the fact)
- Published doctrine (their ideology in polished form)
- Private correspondence (less filtered, more honest)
- Propaganda (designed to recruit — evaluate as such)

## What Success Looks Like

A child who completes this program:

- Has read primary sources from at least 2 revolutionary/decision-maker figures
- Can explain their reasoning from inside their context (empathy)
- Can evaluate their outcomes with the Fruit Test (judgment)
- Can identify patterns across figures (what works, what doesn't, what corrupts)
- Can articulate when revolt is justified and when it isn't — with evidence, not feeling
- Can produce a reasoned response to an impossible scenario
- Is neither a naive pacifist nor a reckless revolutionary
- Has learned to read primary sources instead of accepting pre-digested conclusions
- Understands that the day after victory is harder than the revolution itself

They are not radicalized. They are *informed*. And informed people make better decisions when their own moment of crisis arrives — because life gives the test first.
