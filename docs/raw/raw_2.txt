# Addendum: Local Debate & Civic Argument Slots

## Purpose

This addendum creates reusable debate slots for civic issues that affect the child’s actual place of life.

The goal is not to train partisan argument.

The goal is to teach the child how public decisions are debated, justified, opposed, funded, implemented, remembered, and later judged.

A debate should be tracked when it applies to:

- The town, city, county, state, or country where the child currently lives
- A prior location where the family lived
- A place tied to family heritage
- A jurisdiction where the family owns property, pays taxes, votes, works, studies, worships, or has legal obligations

This keeps civic learning grounded in real responsibility.

---

# Core Principle

A child should learn that government is not merely a set of offices.

Government is also a continuing argument over:

- Power
- Rights
- Duties
- Taxes
- War
- Land
- Schools
- Policing
- Roads
- Courts
- Religion
- Labor
- Property
- Trade
- Citizenship
- Speech
- Public safety
- Public debt
- Public memory

Every generation inherits debates it did not start.

Every generation also creates debates others must live with.

---

# Historical Scope

Debate slots may go back as far as the relevant government existed.

Examples:

- Current county debates
- Founding debates of a town or county
- State constitutional debates
- Colonial-era debates

- Civil War / Reconstruction debates
- School integration debates
- War powers debates
- Taxation debates
- Land-use debates
- Immigration debates
- Church-state debates
- Public debt debates
- Policing and justice debates
- Infrastructure debates

The question is not merely:

**What happened?**

The deeper question is:

**What arguments shaped the world the child inherited?**

Debate Discipline Rules
Steelman Before Criticism

The child must be able to state each major side’s argument in a form that supporters would recognize.

Separate Evidence From Rhetoric

Facts, numbers, laws, documents, and records should be separated from slogans, insults, emotional appeals, and tribal identity.

Track Money

Every debate should ask:

Who pays?
Who profits?
Who owns?
Who funds?
Who receives contracts?
Who receives services?
Who inherits the debt?
Track Power

Every debate should ask:

Who decides?
Who enforces?
Who can block?
Who can delay?
Who can appeal?
Who is ignored?
Track Time

Every debate should ask:

What happens immediately?
What happens in five years?
What happens in a generation?
What burden is handed forward?
No Poisoning

The debate slot should not train the child to hate a party, class, sex, religion, race, region, or generation.

The focus is on arguments, incentives, evidence, institutions, decisions, and consequences.
