# Lesson Plan: Why Veterans Explode Over Small Things (Window of Tolerance)

## Source
YouTube — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v= (Dr. Sharma, Air Force veteran psychiatrist)

## Pillar
Resilience / Character

## Focus Questions
1. What is the "window of tolerance" and how does military service change it?
2. Why can a veteran handle a real emergency calmly but explode over a coffee mug?
3. What is the difference between crisis competence and everyday emotional regulation?
4. Why does the "transmission" analogy work? What are the missing gears?
5. How does this connect to the Life Navigation concept of calibration — your system was built for one environment and now operates in another?

## Key Concepts to Identify
- Window of tolerance (Dr. Dan Siegel): the zone where you can experience stress and still function
- Military training strips the middle gears: you learn park and fifth gear, nothing between
- The window stretches WIDE at the top (catastrophe) but paper-thin at the bottom (daily frustration)
- Crisis competence and daily regulation are two DIFFERENT skill sets built in different parts of the brain
- The shame cycle: overreaction → shame → narrower window → more overreaction
- Naming the pattern interrupts the loop
- Building the "missing gears" through somatic practice (grounding, breathing, 30-second pauses)
- For families: never say "You handled the military, why can't you handle this?" — that pours shame into the gap
- The goal is not to lose crisis competence. The goal is to BUILD the complementary skill.

## Goals
- **Rebrief:** Explain the window of tolerance concept and the transmission analogy in your own words. Why does a system calibrated for catastrophe fail at handling Tuesday?
- **Expansion:** Think about calibration in your own life. Where is YOUR system calibrated too hot or too cold for the environment you are actually in? This applies beyond military — any high-pressure environment (competitive sports, intense academics, family crisis) can strip gears the same way. What are your "missing gears"? Connect to the Resilience pillar's ADAPT framework and the Life Navigation concept of recognizing when endurance has become destruction.
- **Teachback:** Explain to a family member why "overreacting to small things" might not be weakness but miscalibration. Teach them the transmission analogy. Ask: have they seen this pattern in anyone they know? What would "meeting someone there without judgment" look like in practice?
